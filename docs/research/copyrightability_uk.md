# UK Copyrightability and Authorship of AI-Assisted Creative Work

Research brief for a talk to MA Digital Design students, University of York. All sources accessed 2026-04-21. Currency notes flag items liable to change before the talk.

---

## 1. UK copyrightability of AI-generated vs AI-assisted work

### Summary

UK copyright law currently distinguishes three scenarios:

1. **AI-assisted works** (human creates, AI is a tool). Protected as normal literary/artistic works if the "author's own intellectual creation" test is met. Full life-plus-70-years term. Human is the author.
2. **Computer-generated works with no human author (s.9(3) CDPA 1988)**. Protected for 50 years from creation. Author is deemed to be "the person by whom the arrangements necessary for the creation of the work are undertaken" (s.9(3)). Whether prompt-only AI outputs genuinely fall here is contested and untested in court.
3. **Purely AI-generated works with no meaningful human input**. Most commentators, and the UK government's March 2026 report, consider these likely unprotected (or protectable only, and precariously, via s.9(3)).

The originality threshold that matters for the first category is the EU-derived "author's own intellectual creation" standard, imported into UK law via *Infopaq* and still applied post-Brexit. Arnold LJ re-confirmed this in *THJ v Sheridan* [2023] (see IPKat below). It requires expressive choices reflecting the author's "personal touch" -- mere skill, labour or technical effort is not enough. In the AI-assisted context, that means the human must make meaningful creative decisions about selection, arrangement, composition, editing, or post-processing of the work. Writing a short prompt and accepting the model's first output is very unlikely to clear this bar.

s.9(3) itself is a 1988 UK-specific oddity: it predates modern generative AI and was written with things like procedurally generated maps in mind. It has never been authoritatively tested against a prompt-to-image model. The government's March 2026 report (see section 2) explicitly proposes removing s.9(3) in the absence of evidence of its ongoing value, while retaining normal copyright for AI-assisted works.

### Currency note

**In flux.** The March 2026 government report proposes removing s.9(3) but introduces no legislation and gives no timetable. Expect no change to the statute before April 2026, but the policy signal is clear: treat s.9(3) as legally live but politically shaky. The originality threshold for AI-assisted work is judge-made and evolving; no UK court has yet ruled on how much human input is "enough" in a generative AI context.

### What I could not find

An authoritative numerical or procedural test for how much human creative contribution crosses the originality threshold for AI-assisted work. There is no UK case on point. Commentary converges on "meaningful expressive choices" but does not operationalise this for, e.g., prompt length, number of iterations, or extent of post-editing.

## 2. Recent UK court rulings (2024-2026) on AI authorship / s.9(3)

### Summary

There is still no UK judgment directly settling whether generative AI outputs attract copyright or how s.9(3) applies to them.

The headline 2025 case is **Getty Images (US) Inc & ors v Stability AI Ltd [2025] EWHC 2863 (Ch)**, handed down November 2025. It concerned the training and distribution of Stable Diffusion, not the authorship of its outputs. The court held:

- Model weights are not themselves "infringing copies" because they do not contain or embody the copyright works, even transiently.
- Getty's training-infringement claims were withdrawn at trial for want of evidence of the relevant acts occurring in the UK (copyright being territorial).

The judgment is **silent** on (a) whether AI-generated images attract copyright, (b) how s.9(3) applies, and (c) what human input is required for an AI-assisted work to be protected. Commentators treat it as a narrow, pro-AI-industry result on infringement by models, not a statement on authorship.

No other 2024-2026 UK ruling squarely addresses authorship of AI-assisted output.

### Evidence / citations

- Sidley Austin, "The UK's First Copyright vs. AI Decision": <https://www.sidley.com/en/insights/newsupdates/2025/11/uk-first-copyright-vs-ai-decision-key-takeaways-on-a-win-for-the-ai-industry> (accessed 2026-04-21).
- Hogan Lovells, "UK High Court: landmark decision on copyright and Gen AI": <https://www.hoganlovells.com/en/publications/getty-loses-uk-copyright-case-against-stability-ai-> (accessed 2026-04-21).
- DWF, "The first major UK ruling on generative AI and IP rights": <https://dwfgroup.com/en/news-and-insights/insights/2025/11/the-first-major-uk-ruling-on-generative-ai-and-ip-rights> (accessed 2026-04-21).

### Currency note

**Stable for now, but watch for appeal.** Getty has indicated dissatisfaction; an appeal is plausible but unlikely to conclude before the talk. No other major authorship case is known to be pending judgment. If a new ruling lands between now and the talk, it most likely concerns training-data infringement, not authorship.

### Conflicts / disagreements

Commentators broadly agree on what the Getty judgment does and does not say. Disagreement is on implications: some (e.g. Sidley) frame it as a clear AI-industry win; others (e.g. DWF, Hogan Lovells) emphasise its narrowness and that the core training-data question in the UK remains unresolved.

## 3. University of York guidance on student use of generative AI

### Summary

York operates two layers of guidance:

1. **Policy on Acceptable Assistance with Assessments (including Generative AI, Proofreading and Translation)** - the formal policy, carried over from 2023/24 into 2024/25 and referenced in the 2025/26 assessment policy.
2. **Student-facing guidance pages** under "Assessment and examination" and the Subject Guides "Generative AI: a Practical Guide".

The core rules for students:

- **Declare AI use.** For most assessments, any use of generative AI in the production of assessed work must be declared, unless the module/assessment brief says otherwise. The declaration is expected to describe what tool was used, how, and to what extent.
- **Don't substitute AI for your own thinking.** AI may be used to explore concepts, clarify vocabulary, suggest resources, or generate ideas, but not to generate the assessed work itself.
- **Avoid "false authorship".** Submitting work where AI, not the student, is the effective author is academic misconduct.
- **Preferred tool: Google Gemini via York credentials**, because York has a data-protection arrangement that keeps inputs out of training and human review.
- **Never input personal, sensitive, or copyrighted material** into public generative AI tools.
- **Module-level rules override the default.** Individual modules may permit more, or less, AI use; students must check the brief.

Students may be asked to attend a panel and explain how they produced their work if misconduct is suspected.

## 4. Broader UK HE sector guidance

### Summary

Three sector-level reference points are relevant:

**Russell Group principles (July 2023, maintained to January 2025).** Five principles: AI literacy; staff development; adaptation of teaching and assessment; upholding academic rigour and integrity; collaborative sharing of practice. The principles commit signatory universities (including York) to embed ethical AI use, but they do **not** prescribe a specific declaration format. They leave the operational details to each institution.

**QAA.** Since 2023 QAA has published advice on assessment integrity in the generative AI era, including "Maintaining quality and standards in the ChatGPT era" and "Reconsidering assessment for the ChatGPT era" (both 2023, still referenced). In 2025 QAA funded collaborative enhancement projects (e.g. SOAS-led toolkit) on secure, inclusive assessment design. QAA's posture is pro-transparent-use: it urges providers to teach students to use AI critically, not to ban it.

**Jisc.** Jisc runs ongoing work on AI in FE/HE assessment, including a year-long AI-in-assessment project from September 2025 trialling both marking-support tools and general-purpose tools, and regular "Student perceptions of AI" reporting (2025 edition drawing on 1,274 survey responses). Jisc's framing is the "avoid / outrun / embrace" triad, with current advice leaning towards embrace-with-guardrails.

Accepted sector practice in April 2026 is therefore:

- **Declare any use** of generative AI in assessed work, and describe it specifically (tool, purpose, extent).
- **Treat AI as a tool, not an author.** Substantive creative and analytical work must be the student's own.
- **Module-level instructions rule.** Default "declare" rules can be loosened or tightened by individual assessment briefs.

## 5. Professional body positions on AI in portfolios and competition entries

### Summary

The clearest, most enforceable current position comes from **D&AD**, which applies to any student or graduating designer considering competition entry:

- For the **2026 D&AD Awards**, entrants must provide a **structured disclosure of any AI involvement at the point of entry, across all categories**. This extends beyond the creative output to cover all submission materials, including case films and supporting documentation.
- Entries that use AI are **eligible, provided a human creator is responsible for the work** and all other eligibility criteria are met.
- Each entry must be accompanied by an **Entry Validation Card**, signed by a senior representative of the submitting agency/company or by the freelance lead, confirming the declarations are accurate.
- "Use of AI & Technology" subcategories exist across all categories.

This is best read as the current industry benchmark: if something is good enough for D&AD submission, it is good enough for most UK portfolio and client contexts.

For **portfolios more generally**, there is no single UK professional body dictating rules (the Design Council and D&AD publish guidance but not binding rules for unpublished portfolio work). Current sector best-practice, repeated across 2025 industry commentary, is:

- Treat AI as a **named collaborator, not a ghost**. List the tools used (Midjourney, Runway, ChatGPT, etc.), the role they played (ideation, asset generation, copywriting, motion, upscaling), and what the human did.
- Show the process. A portfolio entry that demonstrates prompt iteration, selection, editing, and integration is more legible and more credible than a glossy AI output presented as if hand-made.
- Be alert to **training-data provenance** of the tools used; client and competition terms increasingly require this.

- D&AD, "What's new" (2026 changes): <https://www.dandad.org/awards/d-ad-awards/whats-new> (accessed 2026-04-21).

### What I could not find

A UK professional body with a binding, universal rulebook for AI attribution in personal portfolios. The RSA, Design Council, and AGDA-equivalents do not publish such a document. Guidance is competition-led (D&AD being the most concrete) and client-led.

## Practical rubric: can I claim this? what do I need to declare?

A student can apply these decision rules to any piece of work:

**Step 1 - Assess human creative contribution.**

- Did I make substantive expressive choices (concept, composition, selection, editing, integration, narrative, typography, interaction design)?
- Could another person, given the same tool and a short prompt, have produced something meaningfully the same?

If the answers are **yes / no**, treat the work as AI-assisted with you as the author. If the answers are **no / yes**, treat the work as AI-generated; your copyright claim is weak and possibly non-existent.

**Step 2 - Decide what to declare.**

- **Every** use of generative AI in assessed work at York must be declared by default. Module briefs can override.
- Declarations should name the tool, say what it was used for (ideation / asset generation / copy / code / upscaling / etc.), and describe the extent (one image, 20 variants narrowed to one, etc.).
- If you cannot comfortably describe how AI was used, you probably used it too much.

**Step 3 - Decide what you can claim.**

- **AI-assisted with real human input** -> claim authorship normally; copyright subsists; you can license, sell, or submit to D&AD with disclosure.
- **Prompt-only output you barely touched** -> do not claim authorship; if you include it in a portfolio, label it clearly as generated and describe its role (e.g. reference, mood board, exploratory).
- **Work built from other people's copyrighted assets via AI** -> assume risk; check tool terms; for client work, don't.

**Step 4 - Competition and client work.**

- Assume full disclosure is required. Use the D&AD 2026 structured-disclosure model as your baseline: what was AI, where, how much, and who (human) is responsible.
- If a client or competition forbids AI, "I only used it for ideation" is still AI use and still needs to be declared or excluded.

**Step 5 - Be careful with what you feed in.**

- No personal data, no confidential client material, no third-party copyrighted assets in public tools. York recommends Google Gemini via university credentials for protected handling.

## What this means for our talk

Four plain-language rules for MA Digital Design students:

1. **You are the author only if you did the thinking.** UK copyright law rewards human creative choices, not prompt-writing. If a stranger with the same tool could have made it, it isn't really yours.
2. **Declare AI use every time, in detail.** York requires it by default, the Russell Group and QAA expect it, and D&AD makes it mandatory for competition entry. Say which tool, what it did, and how much. Vague declarations read as hiding something.
3. **Treat AI as a named collaborator in your portfolio.** Show the human work: concept, selection, iteration, edit. A transparent AI-assisted case study is a stronger portfolio piece than a polished AI-generated image pretending to be hand-made.
4. **The law is moving; your habits should be robust to that.** s.9(3) may be repealed; disclosure rules will tighten, not loosen. Build the habit of over-documenting your process now, and you will be safe whichever way the rules go.
